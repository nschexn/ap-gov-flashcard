AP U.S. Government & Politics Flashcards

Declaration of Independence
Document asserting American independence from Britain, emphasizing natural rights and popular sovereignty.
Ideals of Democracy
Core principles including liberty, equality, and justice that guide democratic governance.
Principles of Limited Government
Government structure where power is restricted by law, often through a constitution.
Popular Sovereignty
The idea that political power resides with the people.
Republicanism
A philosophy of limited government with elected representatives serving at the will of the people.
Social Contract
Theory that individuals consent to form a government that protects their rights.
Natural Rights
Fundamental rights such as life, liberty, and property, believed to be granted by nature or God.
Federalism
System of government where power is divided between national and state governments.
Dual Federalism
Doctrine that federal and state governments are co-equals, each sovereign in their own sphere.
Cooperative Federalism
Concept where national, state, and local governments interact cooperatively to solve problems.
New Federalism
Political philosophy of devolution, transferring powers back to the states.
Federal Supremacy
Federal laws and Constitution take precedence over state laws (Supremacy Clause).
Articles of Confederation
First U.S. constitution, created a weak central government.
Constitutional Convention
1787 meeting to revise the Articles, resulted in the U.S. Constitution.
Amending the Constitution
Process outlined in Article V for changing the Constitution.
Ratification of the Constitution
Approval process by which the Constitution was adopted by the states.
Legislative Branch
Makes laws; consists of the House of Representatives and Senate.
Executive Branch
Enforces laws; headed by the President.
Judiciary Branch
Interprets laws; consists of the Supreme Court and other federal courts.
Constitutionalism
Principle that government should be limited by a constitution.
Unicameralism
A legislature with one chamber.
Bicameralism
A legislature with two chambers.
Virginia Plan
Proposal for a bicameral legislature based on population.
New Jersey Plan
Proposal for equal representation in a unicameral legislature.
Connecticut Compromise
Combined Virginia and New Jersey Plans; created bicameral Congress.
Three-Fifths Compromise
Agreement to count slaves as 3/5 of a person for representation.
Federalists
Supporters of the Constitution and strong central government.
Anti-Federalists
Opponents of the Constitution, favored states' rights.
Natural Law
Philosophy asserting certain rights are inherent by human nature.
Separation of Powers
Division of government responsibilities into distinct branches.
Checks and Balances
System ensuring no branch becomes too powerful.
Divided Government
When different parties control different branches of government.
Enumerated Powers
Powers explicitly granted to Congress in the Constitution.
Implied Powers
Powers not explicitly stated but inferred from the Constitution.
Inherent Powers
Powers that belong to the national government by virtue of being sovereign.
Concurrent Powers
Powers shared by both federal and state governments.
State's Rights
Political powers reserved for the state governments.
Block Grants
Federal funds given to states for broad purposes.
Categorical Grants
Federal funds for specific purposes with conditions.
Project Grants
Federal grants for specific projects based on applications.
Unfunded Mandate
Federal requirements without funding support.
Conservatism
Political ideology favoring limited government and traditional values.
Socialism
Political ideology advocating for social ownership and egalitarianism.
Libertarianism
Political philosophy emphasizing individual liberty and minimal government.
Liberalism
Political ideology favoring government intervention for social justice.
Shays' Rebellion
Uprising highlighting weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation.
Direct Democracy
Citizens vote directly on laws and policies.
Representative Democracy
Citizens elect representatives to make decisions.
Constitutional Democracy
Democracy governed by a constitution.
Theocracy
Government ruled by religious leaders or principles.
Totalitarianism
Centralized government with total control over public and private life.
Authoritarianism
Government with strong central power and limited political freedoms.
Anarchism
Belief in the abolition of all government.
Popular Consent
Government derives power from the consent of the governed.
Civil War Amendments
13th (abolished slavery), 14th (equal protection), 15th (voting rights).
Electoral College
System for electing the U.S. President via state electors.
Brutus I
Anti-Federalist paper warning against a strong central government.
Federalist Papers
Essays promoting ratification of the Constitution.
Federalist 10
Argues for a large republic to control factions.
Federalist 51
Explains checks and balances and separation of powers.
Federalist 21
Discusses weaknesses of the Articles and need for stronger union.
McCulloch v. Maryland
Established federal supremacy and implied powers.
U.S. v. Lopez
Limited Congress's power under the Commerce Clause.
U.S. Constitution
Supreme law of the United States.
Supremacy Clause
Federal law supersedes state law.
Necessary and Proper Clause
Allows Congress to make laws required to execute its powers.
Commerce Clause
Grants Congress power to regulate interstate commerce.
Due Process Clause
14th Amendment clause guaranteeing legal fairness.
Equal Protection Clause
14th Amendment clause requiring equal treatment under the law.
Amendment Process
Outlined in Article V; allows changes to the Constitution.
Bill of Rights
First ten amendments guaranteeing individual freedoms.
John Locke
Philosopher advocating natural rights and social contract.
Thomas Hobbes
Philosopher advocating strong government to avoid chaos.
Articles of the Constitution
Seven articles outlining the structure and powers of government.
Branches of Government
Legislative, Executive, and Judicial branches.
Houses of Congress
House of Representatives and Senate.
Madisonian Conception of Government
Government structure to control factions and prevent tyranny.
Madisonian Conception of Faction
Groups with interests contrary to the rights of others or the common good.
